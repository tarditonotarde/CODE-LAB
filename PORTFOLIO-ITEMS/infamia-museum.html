Infamis is a digital archive dedicated to the figures who have shaped history through corruption, deception, and unethical behavior. By curating a collection of artistic expressions inspired by these infamous characters, the project transforms outrage into creative resistance.

Concept & Approach

Infamis challenges the idea that art should only reflect beauty and virtue. Instead, it embraces anger as a valid and powerful emotion, using visual storytelling to expose injustice. The project serves as both a critique and a reminder that power ultimately belongs to the people, echoing the message of Patti Smith’s song, People Have the Power.

Curatorial Themes

  • Scoundrels & Thieves: Artistic exploration of historical and contemporary figures known for corruption and betrayal.
  • The Politics of Anger: How frustration and rage can be transformed into impactful artistic statements.
  • Power & Resistance: Highlighting how art has historically been used as a form of protest and activism.
  • Digital Narratives: Use of online platforms to create an accessible, interactive, and evolving archive.

UX Research Insights

1. User Research & Target Audience

Primary Users: Activists, artists, researchers, and individuals interested in social justice and digital archiving.

User Needs: Users seek an engaging, interactive, and informative platform that allows them to explore, contribute, and reflect on historical and contemporary figures of infamy.

Pain Points:

  • Difficulty accessing well-structured information on corrupt figures.
  • Lack of interactive elements in digital archives.
  • Need for a visually compelling interface that enhances storytelling.

2. Usability Testing

Conducted 5 remote usability tests with participants from diverse backgrounds (activists, students, and artists).

  • 80% of users found the navigation intuitive, but some sections needed clearer categorization.
  • Users appreciated the «Hell’s Wall» feature but suggested adding more context on each figure.
  • Some participants felt overwhelmed by text-heavy content and requested more interactive elements (videos, infographics).

3. Accessibility Considerations

  • Ensured high contrast design for readability.
  • Added alt text for all images to improve screen reader compatibility.
  • Improved keyboard navigation to make the site more inclusive.

4. Wireframing & Prototyping

Used Figma for wireframing and conducted A/B testing on homepage layout. Iterated on content hierarchy, ensuring that the most relevant stories were easily accessible.

Visual & Editorial Identity

Infamis combines bold visual aesthetics with raw, expressive storytelling. The project’s identity is shaped by:

  • Mixed Media Artworks: Paintings, digital illustrations, and street art-inspired visuals that capture the intensity of the themes.
  • High-Contrast Graphics: A striking, rebellious aesthetic that reflects the spirit of dissent.
  • Archival Structure: A living, evolving collection where artists and contributors continuously add to the narrative.

The Experience

Visitors to the digital archive are encouraged to interact, contribute, and reflect on the figures and themes presented. The project is not just a static collection but a call to awareness and action—reminding viewers that corruption thrives in silence, and art can be a tool to challenge it.

Next Steps

  • Enhance interactivity: Implement hover-triggered tooltips for quick information access.
  • Expand multimedia content: Introduce short-form video narratives.
  • Improve content categorization: Use tags and filters for better content discovery.

Conclusion

Infamis reclaims anger as a force for artistic and social transformation. By documenting and exposing figures of infamy, the project serves as both a critique of power and an assertion of collective agency. The people still have the power—it’s time to use it.
